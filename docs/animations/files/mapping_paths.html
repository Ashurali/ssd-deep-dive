Mapping lookup paths

Where does the map table live — and what does that cost on every single read? Four architectures race the same 4 KB request.

Warm-up: why page mapping exists at all

Block mapping can't point at a single page — updating one page means read the whole block, modify, write the whole block elsewhere. That's the ten-second explanation of USB-stick random-write tragedy, and why SSDs pay for page-granularity tables. (Figs 4-3→4-5)

The race

DRAM drive: lookup in onboard DRAM (~0.1 µs) → one flash read. DRAM-less: the second-level map lives in flash — a random read misses the tiny SRAM cache, so the drive does a flash read to fetch the mapping, then a flash read for the data: two long bars, half the effective speed (Fig 4-13). HMB: the map sits in borrowed host RAM — a PCIe hop (~1 µs) is slower than DRAM but ~40× faster than a flash read. Switch to sequential and DRAM-less catches up: one cached map chunk serves many neighboring pages. HPB goes further — the host sends the physical address with the command, and the device skips its lookup entirely.
